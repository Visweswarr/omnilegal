ABSTRACT 
 
The rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence, particularly in the field of Generative AI, has 
opened new possibilities for automating complex text-based tasks across various domains, 
including healthcare. Medical reports such as laboratory results and prescriptions contain 
detailed information with complex terminology and numerical data that are often difficult for 
patients to interpret without professional assistance. This project presents the design, 
development, and deployment of a domain-specific Large Language Model (LLM) application 
aimed at automating the analysis and generation of structured medical report summaries. The 
proposed system accepts medical documents in the form of PDF or image files and processes 
them using a multimodal LLM to extract relevant information and generate structured outputs. 
These outputs include patient-friendly summaries, key findings, risk level classification, and 
actionable recommendations. The system is implemented using Python and deployed through 
a web-based interface, enabling easy accessibility and interaction for users. Prompt engineering 
techniques are employed to guide the model in generating consistent and domain-specific 
responses without the need for extensive fine-tuning. To evaluate the performance of the 
system, quantitative metrics such as ROUGE and BLEU are used to compare the AI-generated 
outputs with human-verified reference data. The results demonstrate that the model effectively 
captures essential medical information and produces meaningful summaries, although 
variations in phrasing affect precision-based metrics like BLEU. Overall, the project highlights 
the potential of domain-specific LLM applications in improving accessibility, efficiency, and 
understanding of medical information, while also emphasizing the importance of evaluation in 
validating generative AI systems.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background 
In recent years, the rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence has significantly transformed 
various domains, with healthcare emerging as one of the most impactful areas of application. 
Among the many developments in AI, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated 
remarkable capability in understanding, processing, and generating human-like text. These 
models are trained on vast amounts of data and can interpret complex language patterns, 
making them highly suitable for tasks involving unstructured textual information. In the 
healthcare domain, a large portion of data exists in textual form, such as laboratory reports, 
prescriptions, and diagnostic summaries. 
1.2 Motivation 
The motivation behind this project arises from the challenges faced by individuals when 
attempting to understand their medical reports without professional assistance. Most patients 
lack the medical knowledge required to interpret test values, identify abnormal parameters, or 
understand the implications of certain medical terms. This often leads to confusion, anxiety, or 
misinterpretation of health conditions. Additionally, healthcare professionals are frequently 
burdened with heavy workloads, limiting the time they can dedicate to explaining reports in 
detail to each patient. In rural and underserved areas, access to qualified medical professionals 
is further restricted, making it even more difficult for individuals to receive proper guidance.  
1.3 Problem Statement 
Medical reports are inherently complex, containing numerical values, abbreviations, and 
domain-specific terminology such as HbA1c, LDL cholesterol, TSH levels, and various 
biochemical indicators. For a non-medical user, interpreting these values and understanding 
their significance is a difficult task. Traditional systems either display reports in their original 
format or provide limited assistance, without offering meaningful interpretation or personalized 
insights. Furthermore, manual analysis by healthcare professionals is time-consuming and may 
not always be readily available. This creates a need for an automated system that can process 
medical documents, extract relevant information, and generate understandable summaries. The 
problem addressed in this project is the development of a domain-specific LLM-based 
application that can accept medical reports in formats such as PDF or images, analyze their 
content, generate structured outputs including summaries and risk levels, and evaluate the 

 
quality of generated text using quantitative metrics like ROUGE and BLEU in comparison with 
human-verified reference data. 
1.4 Objectives 
The primary objective of this project is to design and implement a domain-specific Large 
Language Model application that automates the process of medical report analysis and text 
generation. The system aims to support multiple types of medical documents, including 
laboratory reports and prescriptions, and generate structured outputs such as patient-friendly 
summaries, key findings, risk classifications, and recommendations. Another important 
objective is to deploy the application using a user-friendly interface so that it can be accessed 
easily by users without technical expertise. In addition to functionality, the project also focuses 
on evaluating the performance of the model using established metrics such as ROUGE and 
BLEU to measure linguistic accuracy and content retention. By comparing AI-generated 
outputs with human-written references, the system’s effectiveness can be quantitatively 
assessed. Overall, the project seeks to demonstrate how generative AI can be applied to solve 
real-world healthcare problems by improving accessibility, understanding, and efficiency in 
medical data interpretation. 
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE SURVEY 
2.1 Existing Systems 
The application of Artificial Intelligence in healthcare has led to the development of several 
systems aimed at assisting medical professionals and improving patient outcomes. One of the 
notable systems is IBM Watson Health, which was designed to support clinical decision-
making by analyzing large volumes of medical data. Although it demonstrated strong 
capabilities, it was primarily enterprise-focused and not accessible for general public use. 
Similarly, Google developed MedPaLM, a specialized language model trained for medical 
question answering, which showcased high accuracy in understanding clinical queries. 
However, its availability is limited and it is not designed for direct deployment by students or 
small-scale developers. General-purpose models like ChatGPT are widely used for text 
generation tasks, but they are not specifically tailored for structured medical report analysis 
and may lack domain-specific consistency. Additionally, services such as Amazon Comprehend 
Medical focus mainly on extracting medical entities from text rather than generating 
meaningful summaries or recommendations. Most existing hospital systems and report portals 

 
only provide digital access to reports without any intelligent interpretation, leaving users to 
rely on manual understanding. 
2.2 Limitations of Existing Systems 
Despite the availability of advanced AI tools, several limitations persist in current systems. 
Many platforms are either restricted to enterprise environments or require significant financial 
investment, making them inaccessible for academic or individual use. General-purpose 
language models often generate unstructured responses, which may not align with the need for 
standardized outputs such as JSON-based medical summaries. Furthermore, most existing tools 
do not support multimodal inputs like PDFs and images simultaneously, which is essential for 
real-world medical report processing. Another major limitation is the absence of risk 
classification and personalized recommendations, which are critical for helping users 
understand the severity of their health conditions. In addition, existing systems rarely 
incorporate evaluation mechanisms such as ROUGE and BLEU metrics, making it difficult to 
quantitatively assess the quality of generated outputs.  
2.3 Research Gap 
From the analysis of existing systems, it is evident that there is a lack of a unified solution that 
combines accessibility, structured output generation, multimodal input handling, and 
quantitative evaluation. Most available tools either focus on information extraction or provide 
general conversational responses, but do not offer a complete pipeline for medical report 
analysis. There is also a noticeable absence of systems that are freely deployable and suitable 
for academic projects while still maintaining a reasonable level of performance. Another 
important gap is the lack of integration between text generation and evaluation metrics, which 
are essential for validating the effectiveness of AI-generated content. 
2.4 Base Model Selection 
To address the identified gaps, an appropriate base model must be selected that satisfies the 
requirements of performance, accessibility, and ease of integration. In this project, the model 
chosen is Gemini 2.5 Flash developed by Google DeepMind. This model offers several 
advantages, including native support for multimodal inputs such as PDF and image files, which 
eliminates the need for additional preprocessing steps like Optical Character Recognition. It is 
capable of generating structured outputs, which is essential for producing consistent and 
interpretable results. Another key advantage is the availability of a free API tier, making it 
suitable for academic use without significant cost constraints. The model also demonstrates 

 
strong performance in understanding medical terminology and generating contextually relevant 
responses. Furthermore, it can be easily integrated into Python-based applications using the 
official SDK, allowing seamless development and deployment. These features make Gemini 
2.5 Flash an appropriate choice for building a domain-specific LLM application focused on 
medical report analysis. 
CHAPTER 3: REQUIREMENTS 
3.1 Hardware Requirements 
The successful implementation and deployment of the proposed system require a basic 
computing environment that is accessible and does not demand high-end infrastructure. The 
memory requirement is relatively low, with a minimum of 4 GB RAM being adequate, although 
8 GB is recommended for better performance and multitasking. Storage requirements are 
minimal, as the project mainly involves code files and lightweight datasets, requiring 
approximately 500 MB of free disk space. 
3.2 Software Requirements 
The software requirements for this project are centered around creating a flexible, scalable, and 
user-friendly application environment. The core programming language used is Python, chosen 
for its simplicity, extensive library support, and strong ecosystem for AI and data processing 
tasks. The user interface is developed using the Streamlit framework, which enables rapid 
development of interactive web applications with minimal effort. For evaluation purposes, the 
rouge-score library is employed to compute ROUGE metrics, which measure content 
similarity, while the NLTK library is used to calculate BLEU scores, which evaluate the 
precision of generated text. Version control is managed using Git and GitHub, enabling 
efficient collaboration and code maintenance. Finally, the application is deployed using 
Streamlit Community Cloud, which provides a free and convenient platform for hosting and 
sharing the application. Together, these software components create a robust and efficient 
environment for developing and deploying the domain-specific LLM application. 
CHAPTER 4: SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE 
4.1 Overview of the System 
The system architecture of the proposed application is designed to provide a seamless flow 
from user input to AI-generated output while maintaining modularity and scalability. The 

 
architecture follows a layered approach consisting of the user interface layer, processing layer, 
model interaction layer, and evaluation layer. The user interacts with the system through a web-
based interface, where medical reports can be uploaded in the form of PDF or image files. Once 
the input is received, the system processes the document, extracts relevant content, and sends 
it to a domain-specific Large Language Model for analysis. The model generates structured 
outputs, which are then validated, enhanced with additional processing such as risk detection, 
and displayed to the user in an understandable format. In parallel, an evaluation pipeline is used 
to assess the quality of the generated output by comparing it with reference data using ROUGE 
and BLEU metrics. This architecture ensures that the system is both functional and measurable 
in terms of performance. 
4.2 Architecture Description 
The architecture begins with the user interface, which is implemented using a web framework 
that allows users to upload files and interact with the system. Once a document is uploaded, it 
is passed to the extraction module, which reads the file content and converts it into a suitable 
format for further processing. This step prevents irrelevant or incorrect inputs from being 
processed further. After classification, the content is sent to the analysis module, where the 
Large Language Model processes the data based on predefined prompts. The model generates 
a structured response that includes key findings, summaries, risk levels, and recommendations. 
Following validation, additional processing is performed, such as identifying important 
medical terms and determining the overall risk level based on predefined conditions. The 
processed output is then displayed to the user through the interface, where it is organized into 
different sections for easy understanding. Additionally, the system includes a chat component 
that allows users to ask follow-up questions based on the uploaded report. Alongside the main 
processing pipeline, an evaluation module operates separately, taking AI-generated outputs and 
comparing them with human-written references to compute performance metrics. This 
comprehensive architecture ensures that the system is capable of handling real-world inputs 
while maintaining reliability and usability. 
4.3 Components Description 
The system is composed of several interconnected components, each responsible for a specific 
function within the overall workflow. The document extractor is responsible for reading and 
converting input files into a format suitable for processing. The analyzer component interacts 
with the Large Language Model to generate structured outputs based on the extracted content.  

 
CHAPTER 5: METHODOLOGY 
5.1 System Design 
The methodology adopted in this project is centered around building a structured and modular 
pipeline that transforms raw medical documents into meaningful, user-friendly insights. The 
system is designed in such a way that it can handle real-world inputs while maintaining clarity, 
accuracy, and scalability. The overall design follows a two-phase approach consisting of an 
analysis phase and an evaluation phase. In the analysis phase, the system accepts medical 
reports in the form of PDF or image files, processes the input, and generates structured outputs 
such as summaries, key findings, and risk levels. In the evaluation phase, the generated outputs 
are compared with human-written reference data to measure the performance of the model 
using quantitative metrics.  
5.2 Model Selection 
The choice of the underlying model plays a crucial role in determining the performance of the 
system. In this project, a domain-specific adaptation is achieved using the Gemini 2.5 Flash 
model, which is capable of handling both textual and visual inputs. The model is selected due 
to its ability to process multimodal data, generate structured responses, and provide fast 
inference. Unlike traditional machine learning models that require extensive training and fine-
tuning, this approach leverages prompt engineering to guide the model’s behavior.  
5.3 Prompt Engineering 
Prompt engineering is a critical aspect of this methodology, as it enables the model to produce 
domain-specific outputs without explicit retraining. Carefully designed prompts are used to 
instruct the model on how to interpret the input and generate responses in a structured format. 
Different prompts are created for different tasks, such as document classification, lab report 
analysis, prescription analysis, and interactive chat responses. 
5.4 Response Generation Process 
The response generation process begins with the extraction of content from the uploaded 
document, which is then passed to a classification module to determine whether the document 
is relevant to the medical domain. Once validated, the content is sent to the model along with 
the appropriate prompt based on the selected mode, such as lab report or prescription analysis. 
Additional processing steps are performed to enhance the output, such as identifying key 
medical terms and determining the overall risk level. The final output is then displayed to the 

 
user in a clear and organized manner. This multi-step process ensures that the system produces 
reliable and meaningful results from unstructured input data. 
5.5 Evaluation Process (ROUGE and BLEU Metrics) 
To assess the quality of the generated outputs, the system incorporates a quantitative evaluation 
process using ROUGE and BLEU metrics. These metrics are widely used in natural language 
processing tasks to measure the similarity between machine-generated text and human-written 
reference text. ROUGE focuses on recall by evaluating how much of the reference content is 
captured in the generated output, while BLEU focuses on precision by measuring how closely 
the generated text matches the reference in terms of word usage. In this project, multiple 
variants of ROUGE, such as ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L, are used to capture 
different levels of similarity, including word-level overlap, phrase-level overlap, and sequence 
matching. BLEU score is computed using smoothing techniques to handle cases where exact 
matches are limited. The evaluation process involves generating outputs for a set of predefined 
samples and comparing them with corresponding reference outputs. This evaluation not only 
validates the effectiveness of the system but also helps identify areas for improvement in 
prompt design and response generation. 
CHAPTER 6: IMPLEMENTATION AND DEPLOYMENT 
6.1 Implementation Overview 
The implementation of the proposed system focuses on developing a functional, user-friendly, 
and modular application that integrates a Large Language Model for medical report analysis. 
The entire system is built using Python, which provides a flexible and efficient environment 
for handling data processing, API integration, and web application development. The 
implementation is divided into multiple modules to ensure clarity, maintainability, and 
scalability. Each module is responsible for a specific task, such as file extraction, document 
analysis, validation, risk detection, and user interaction. By adopting a modular approach, the 
system becomes easier to debug, extend, and optimize in the future. 
6.2 Tools and Technologies Used 
The development of this system relies on a combination of modern tools and technologies that 
support both backend processing and frontend interaction. Python serves as the core 
programming language due to its simplicity and extensive support for libraries related to 

 
artificial intelligence and web development. The user interface is developed using Streamlit, 
which allows rapid creation of interactive web applications with minimal code. 
For handling image inputs and processing uploaded files, the Pillow library is used, which 
supports various image formats and simplifies file manipulation. For evaluation purposes, the 
rouge-score library is used to compute ROUGE metrics, while the NLTK library is used for 
calculating BLEU scores. Git and GitHub are used for version control, allowing efficient 
tracking of changes and collaboration. Together, these tools provide a comprehensive 
environment for building and deploying the application. 
6.3 Code Implementation Details 
The implementation involves several key functionalities that work together to deliver the final 
output. The document classification function is responsible for determining whether the 
uploaded file is a valid medical document. This step prevents unnecessary processing of 
irrelevant inputs. Once the document is validated, the analysis function sends the content to the 
model along with the appropriate prompt, depending on whether the input is a lab report or a 
prescription. The model then generates a structured response, which is parsed and converted 
into a usable format. The validation module ensures that the generated output adheres to a 
predefined schema and contains all required fields. If any inconsistencies are detected, 
appropriate measures are taken to handle them. 
6.4 Deployment Process 
The deployment of the application is carried out using Streamlit Community Cloud, which 
provides a free and convenient platform for hosting web applications. The source code of the 
project is first uploaded to a GitHub repository, which acts as the central storage for all project 
files. The deployment platform is then connected to the repository, allowing automatic updates 
whenever changes are pushed to the main branch. The entry point of the application is defined 
in a main script file, which initializes the user interface and handles user interactions. 
To ensure secure access to the API, the required API key is stored using the secrets management 
feature provided by the deployment platform. Once deployed, the application is accessible 
through a public URL, enabling users to interact with the system from any device with an 
internet connection. This deployment approach ensures scalability, ease of access, and minimal 
maintenance effort. 

 
6.5 Application Interface 
The user interface of the application is designed to be simple, intuitive, and user-friendly, 
ensuring that even non-technical users can easily interact with the system. The main interface 
includes a sidebar where users can upload medical reports and select the type of analysis they 
wish to perform. Once the file is uploaded and the analysis is triggered, the system processes 
the input and displays the results in a structured format. 
Visual indicators, such as color-coded risk levels, are used to highlight the severity of the 
condition. The interface also includes a chat feature that allows users to ask follow-up questions 
related to the uploaded report, providing an interactive experience. Overall, the application 
interface enhances usability and ensures that the system effectively communicates complex 
medical information in a simplified manner. 
CHAPTER 7: EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS 
7.1 Evaluation Setup 
The experimental evaluation of the proposed system is conducted to measure the effectiveness 
of the Large Language Model in generating accurate and meaningful medical report 
summaries. The evaluation process is designed to compare the AI-generated outputs with 
human-written reference data to ensure reliability and quality. A small dataset consisting of 
representative medical reports is prepared, including common test types such as Complete 
Blood Count (CBC), Lipid Panel, and Thyroid Profile.  
The model used for evaluation is Gemini 2.5 Flash, and the outputs are generated using the 
same prompts defined during the implementation phase. To ensure consistency, preprocessing 
steps such as text normalization and lowercasing are applied before computing evaluation 
metrics. Additionally, risk classification accuracy is evaluated by comparing the predicted risk 
level with the reference risk level for each sample. This setup ensures a balanced assessment 
of both linguistic quality and practical usefulness. 
7.2 Results and Observations 
The results obtained from the evaluation demonstrate the performance of the system across 
different metrics. The ROUGE-1 score, which measures word-level overlap, shows a value of 
approximately 0.5078, indicating that the model successfully captures a significant portion of 
the key terms present in the reference summaries. The ROUGE-2 score, which evaluates 

 
phrase-level similarity, is relatively lower at around 0.1986, suggesting that the model often 
rephrases content rather than directly matching the reference text.  
7.3 Performance Analysis 
The performance of the system highlights both its strengths and areas for improvement. The 
relatively high ROUGE-1 score demonstrates that the model is effective in identifying and 
including important medical terms and concepts in its output. This is particularly important in 
the healthcare domain, where accurate representation of key information is critical. The 
moderate ROUGE-L score indicates that the model is capable of maintaining a logical flow of 
information, even if the exact sentence structure differs from the reference. 
On the other hand, the low ROUGE-2 and BLEU scores suggest that the model frequently 
paraphrases content, which reduces exact n-gram matches. However, this behavior is not 
necessarily a limitation, as it reflects the model’s ability to generate natural and diverse 
language. 
CHAPTER 8: DISCUSSION 
8.1 Interpretation of Results 
The results obtained from the experimental evaluation provide important insights into the 
performance and behavior of the proposed system. The relatively high ROUGE-1 score 
indicates that the model is effective in capturing the essential medical terms and key 
information present in the reference summaries. This suggests that the system is capable of 
identifying critical aspects of medical reports, such as abnormal values, diagnostic indicators, 
and relevant conditions. The moderate ROUGE-L score further demonstrates that the generated 
outputs maintain a logical flow and coherence, even when the sentence structure differs from 
that of the reference text. This is particularly important in ensuring that the generated 
summaries are readable and understandable for users. 
8.2 Comparison with Expected Output 
When comparing the AI-generated outputs with the human-written reference data, it is 
observed that the system successfully captures the core meaning and intent of the reference 
summaries in most cases. The generated outputs include relevant medical terms, appropriate 
risk classifications, and meaningful recommendations, which align closely with the expected 
outcomes. 

 
CHAPTER 9: ADVANTAGES AND LIMITATIONS 
9.1 Advantages 
The proposed system offers several significant advantages that make it a practical and 
impactful solution for medical report analysis. One of the primary benefits is its ability to 
simplify complex medical information into clear and understandable language, making it 
accessible even to users without a medical background. By leveraging a domain-specific Large 
Language Model, the system can accurately identify key findings, interpret medical terms, and 
provide structured summaries, which reduces the dependency on manual interpretation by 
healthcare professionals. This not only saves time but also enhances efficiency in 
understanding medical reports. 
Another important advantage is the system’s ability to process multiple input formats, including 
PDF and image files, which reflects real-world usage where medical reports are often shared 
in different formats. The inclusion of risk classification adds further value by helping users 
quickly understand the severity of their condition, enabling them to take appropriate action. 
The interactive chat feature enhances user experience by allowing follow-up questions, thereby 
providing a more personalized and engaging interface. 
9.2 Limitations 
Despite its advantages, the system has certain limitations that need to be acknowledged. One 
of the primary limitations is the reliance on internet connectivity, as the application depends on 
external API calls to access the Large Language Model. This may restrict usability in areas 
with poor network infrastructure. Another limitation is the relatively small size of the 
evaluation dataset, which may not fully represent the diversity of real-world medical reports. 
A larger dataset would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the system’s performance. 
The evaluation metrics used in the project, particularly BLEU, may not accurately reflect the 
quality of generated text in cases where the model produces paraphrased outputs. Since BLEU 
focuses on exact word matches, it tends to assign lower scores even when the generated content 
is semantically correct. Additionally, the system currently supports only English language 
reports, limiting its applicability in regions where other languages are commonly used. 

 
CHAPTER 10: CONCLUSION AND FUTURE SCOPE 
10.1 Conclusion 
The project successfully demonstrates the design and development of a domain-specific Large 
Language Model application for automated medical report analysis. By integrating generative 
AI techniques with a user-friendly interface, the system is able to process medical documents, 
extract meaningful information, and present it in a structured and understandable format. The 
application effectively handles different types of inputs such as laboratory reports and 
prescriptions, and generates outputs that include summaries, key findings, risk levels, and 
recommendations. This significantly improves the accessibility of medical information, 
especially for users who do not possess a medical background. 
An important aspect of the project is the evaluation of the generated outputs using quantitative 
metrics such as ROUGE and BLEU. These metrics provide a systematic way to assess the 
similarity between AI-generated text and human-written reference data, thereby validating the 
effectiveness of the system. Although certain limitations exist in the evaluation process, the 
results indicate that the model performs well in capturing essential information and generating 
meaningful summaries. Overall, the project highlights the potential of generative AI in 
transforming the way medical information is interpreted and communicated. 
10.2 Future Scope 
While the current system provides a strong foundation, there are several opportunities for 
further enhancement and expansion. One of the key areas for improvement is the fine-tuning 
of the language model using domain-specific datasets. This would help in improving accuracy, 
reducing inconsistencies, and making the outputs more aligned with clinical standards. 
Expanding the evaluation dataset to include a larger and more diverse set of medical reports 
would also lead to more reliable and statistically significant results. 
In terms of practical application, the system can be integrated with hospital management 
systems or electronic medical records to enable real-time data processing without manual 
uploads. The development of a mobile application would further improve accessibility and user 
convenience. Additionally, collaboration with medical professionals for clinical validation 
would enhance the credibility and reliability of the system. By addressing these areas, the 
proposed system can evolve into a more comprehensive and impactful solution in the 
healthcare domain.
